Wakana Akimoto

A tangle of one’s thoughts and mind forms an individual lyric world.

The action of delving into one’s inner world is not obviously as explicit as the outer world. Human beings always look at the outside and depend on their eyes, which merely illustrate physical objects. However, the action that cannot be visualised by others but capable of digging a specific subject knowledge will eventually link to other elements and that could lead to another element of communication.

My study is to illuminate something that is invisible or abstract by capturing things poetically. It also reflects my view on the emotion and my metaphysical mind, which explored the role of words in expressions as well as the meaning of writing poetry and the concept of invisibility.

We choose things in everyday life, but actually, the choice is composed of things that are not selected. People, society and the world, they may always consist of the conditions of being opposite.

To define the shape of space is to make the notion of existence and non-existence significant. To see things from a different aspect is linked to a release of the essence from the matter and a release of the world from space and dimensions.

Alice Grenier-Nebout

She encoutered the artistic world at the age of 3, she spent most of her childhood with her grandmother at her ceramics studio or surrounded by a cinematic environment : her mother who`s an actress or her father working behind the camera.
Being in the atelier of ceramics she was first exposed to the discovery of vivid colours transforming inside ovens, to the touch of different organic materials, building childhood symbols as cakes, castles, little people. She soon started to build her own universe of characters and stories.
She contantly travelled between Canada, France and all around the world to follow each of her parents.
She realised the importance of painting when in Canada with her father, they worked together in a massive wooden structure, realising the emotional role of art in connecting people through paint.
When a teenager, against her mother`s will, she felt the urge to fill her room walls with what later would become her biggest obsession : imaginitive worlds of colour .
She followed this instict and continued her progress into a fine art school till the age of 19.
Receiving proposals of collaborations with designers and fashionists, she created a wide range of patterns and images that were used for all different kinds of purposes.

She then moved to London to study Fine Arts at Central Saint Martins and proposed to make an exhibittion in Paris, she chose to explore the subject of Exode isolated from society in her studio in Britanny.

In this first exhibition opening, she sold a total of 11 pieces.

Riah Jocelyn

1.'They Face Eachother, The African, The Negro, Now The Black-British Putto' Sculpture [http://cargocollective.com/riahjocelyn/They-Face-Eachother-The-African-The-Negro-Now-The-Black-British-Putto]
2.'Touch My Baby, Hair' Performance [http://cargocollective.com/riahjocelyn/Touch-My-Baby-Hair]
3.'TO BE CONTINUED...' Video [http://cargocollective.com/riahjocelyn/TO-BE-CONTINUED]
4.'Alien Christening' Sculpture [http://cargocollective.com/riahjocelyn/Alien-Christening]

I explore notions of becoming, birth and transcendence. Currently investigating the idea of Alien and Other. Why is 'race' linked to where a body situated geographically, when in today's climate, no country is mono-cultural?

The goddess Venus and Putti, continually influence my practice. Venus symbolises a mythic being of birth, and relates to my interests in the birth of mythic tropes and assumptions linked to black bodies; a putti’s ability to transcend from the narrative of painting to the reality intrigue me, as they retain the ability to be liminal.

Critiquing myths through my own mythical imagery, my work centres around digital collage as a method of creating my own utopia. Being able to merge existing imagery is vital to my practice, as collage enables me to piece together an image that critiques imagery of stereotypes and tropes of black people.

Alongside digital collage, sculpture and objects involve the audience within a setting to question traditional societal views. I want to emphasise empathy through my work by enticing my audience to interact with my work, as a mobile from my place of thought to the audiences, to provoke inner discussions.

Xilesia Dwyer Henry


My work takes a variety of forms depending on the subject matter and context. ‘Mental Health, Series 1-3’ (2014-16), is an image-based practice that redacts, dissects, burns out points of identification from personal photographs and diary entries, which are repositories of memory. I use my art as a form of communication; about social exclusion and how we can see it working in material culture. I consider each piece I create a form of an advertisement, which creates an opportunity to draw attention to social issues -where individuals are stigmatized or where education/awareness is needed.
My opinion is that we are reliant upon the media to give us coverage on issues that affect us and the people that we live amongst, while all the time class division increases and tabloid and broadsheets create biased stances on how issues affect us and limit what we feel we can do to fix the problem.
I never promise to be a cure to any issue that I address but I agree with psychologist Erik Erikson, who tells us that as we form ‘in groups’ with people who we as similar, and see larger contrasts between us and those that don’t fit the criteria.
My work is used as a reminder that instinctively we do this subconsciously as a form of socialisation in the materials of our daily life and culture and that we are more similar than we appear. If I can create work that draws attention to this, we might better understand how similar we are as people.

Juliana Dorso

1. Dirty Woolfian Underwear_ Outfits made by painted canvas performed
2. M in GS_ Mixed media painting inspired by unfictional Woolfian territory
3. Reader Digest Of My Beloved Work_ Photograph, print, inks

I am a visual poet, a painter inspired by Virginia Woolf’s books.
My work explores the idea of “A Room of One’s Own”, Woolf’s assertion that to have a room of one’s own is to have the right and the possibility to be independent and have a place which belongs to you to live, to write, to think. My ‘rooms’ are pictorial spaces, painted outfits created in canvas that can speak, walk, present for themselves. I translate this literary concept into a visual field; a place for the imagination, fiction and re-made memories, where it can be seen from different standpoints in the world.

For me, my room of one’s own is the act of painting, and when I share painting with others, through outfits or objects-to-be-performed-by-others, I create a kind of common room of one’s own. This figurative gesture offers a nomadic proposition - we can create by ourselves or with others, it can happen anywhere no matter how constrained.

My work is concerned with creating a dialogue between territories, memories and people in order to create a poetic place. Sometimes it could be a situation made by paintings, sometimes it could be a performance made by outfits, or parades, or animations, words and images. I am open to whatever develops out of my research through talking to people, looking at the world, collecting images, opinions and ideas.
I am inspired by people’s different backgrounds and artistic fields, such as aesthetic poetry, literature, music, cinema, theater and opera. Whatever the medium, I always feel by overlapping layers I can create a painting that offers not a form of re-enactment the opportunity to re-make, re-imagine, re-invent.

Brad Dyer

My process is like a growth, combining my interests in mental health, repetition and therapeutic actions and giving that a physical body.

Over the course of my practice a heavy theme of Copying and Replication have lead me to my fascination with bees and honeycomb, coming across the term trypophobia which Is the fear of holes in things; particularly when confronted with these images, I didn’t feel discomfort but actually satisfaction, the way the eye picks up so much detail and is able to recognise complex patterns within when using clustered holes, it creates something I personally find absorbing.

With my current work I am exploring the opposite of holes and creating bumps, described like scales. Using a prepared surfaced and a combination of predictable placement of the pins to bring the object to life.

“Standing at the edge of another hole, I peer into this vast cavity, this promising wound. Perhaps it is here where the Bear herself lies waiting as another fractured utterance, a hollow in the ground whose empty abandonment brings me closer to her than her self-presence ever could. She is fleeting, and speaking of her with broken voice which cracks under a weight it is unable to sustain, a voice which wobbles and strains at each edge as a hand wavers, trying to catch those words who slip.

JJ Lincoln

My focus has been on subconscious fantasies of authority and rebellion. incorporating myself as subject, i have scrutinized and assaulted my subconcscious in an attempt to confront the irreconcilable. Whether performance, video, painting or sculptural installation, my body of work questions notions of subjectivity, authenticity and authority (both institional and individual).

In acting out rebellious fantasies, I then question those very desires and drives. In this regard, there is a conventional freudian pattern to my creative process of remembering, repeating and working through. Each of these layers is an important part of the whole process of trangsgression and development.

Whether I incoprpate visual imagery from my personal and cultural history as an English boy/man, or that of the institution in which this work is happening (CSM), it is with these institutions that I grapple, and so the visual language shows an explicit confrontation with that.

Cecelia Johnson

1.'Uuuuter', HD Video
2.'Photograph of Nothing', Inkjet Print on Paper
3.'Chair screeches when they move, it turns slightly to the wall, away from the double opening doors, an opening from which a pitch suddenly rose unbearable to noise: ', Digital Print on Paper, 6.7 metres

I get his train every day and I’ve never seen him move like that. Standing by the train door, placing more weight on one leg (his left I think from my memory), his hand touching the curved metal pole which a piece of glass sits in, fixed to the train wall to act as some kind of divider between the door and the seating area.

His fingers would touch then move away from the pole rhythmically as he talked with someone he knew on the train (maybe it was this person that produced the change). It was his left leg, but additionally his left arm was doing this movement, whilst he lent his shoulder on the pole, additionally so his arm ran down the length of the pole. My eye meets his hand. His other arm, swung crossing his left arm (the one in full contact with the pole) meeting at the bicep, lightly grabbing it then once again, SWING.

Inca Jordan

1.'Untitled' Oil on Canvas
2.'Running With Strangers' Pages from publication
3.'Running With Strangers' Pages from publication
4.'Untitled' Digital print

My practice involves an interplay between moving image, text and painting. The screenshot has become a motif in my work and is a dominant and recurring idea that has enabled me to examine questions around surveillance and digital culture. The key themes I aim to address in my practice are surveillance, technology, the internet, online Panopticism and conditioning.

Text and image based work allows me to investigate the role of the internet in contemporary society and examine automated systems online, challenging the tacit complicity of an individual to electronic monitoring.

I use a digital camcorder to capture films from which I then collate screenshots in an archival manner in order to gain material to work alongside text pieces which form a narrative. Paintings then develop the narrative whilst enabling me to disrupt the constancy dictated by technology, reproducing the painterly aspects of glitch in a slow, time consuming medium.

Antoine Langenieux-Villard

1.'There is always a frame' Free standing stretcher / Acrylic and spray paint on sewn fabrics / Mural collage - acrylic on wood mdf - 250 x 250 x 200 cm
2.Installation Big Space VI - Free standing metal stretcher - Acrylic and spray paint on sewn fabrics / Mural collage, mixed media on wood, pins 200 x 450 x 250 cm
3.'Zarathoustra III' Acrylic, ink, oil on polyester - 140 x 180cm
4.'Zarathoustra II' Acrylic, ink , spray on polyester - 140 x 180 cm

It is through our perception that we can represent ourselves in the world. A sensation that is an optical and tactile movement.
Painting allows me to materialise the dialectic between seeing, touching, doing and knowing, considering the eye-hand couple as the main tool of the painter.
Painting is a mode of construction: I'm not attached to a form, but to the conditions of its emergence. Each work carries or destroys the previous gestures.
The layers overlap each other, confusing the plane as the strata of memory. My use of transparent material enables me to paint on both side of the material.
This rotation allows me to avoid the preconception of the final result. I question materiality and technique by playing with and disrupting the elements that traditionally belong to the pictorial plane. By using different systems such as collage and sewing, I bring together elements that carry their own temporality. I aim to maintain ambiguities and tensions, underlying or imposed by the support. The stretcher is a part of the thickness, allowing the behind to become apart of the visible surface. Painting is not a window on the world but a door-window: we go in, we go out.

Kaya Ozard

3. Untitled 26

My practice is drawn to unique, chance encounters with everyday detritus in the street. I choose to work with very specific ‘things’, each of which will dictate a diverse and distinctive approach accompanied by the use of materials I deem appropriate.

When I am creating the work I always contemplate the importance of the viewer ‘double taking’. Some may not even know what exactly it is that they are observing. Through situating my artwork back in the street where I initially found and responded to the detritus, I allow the viewer an unexpected connection to the piece. My reason for placing the work back outside, is so that it has the exact same surroundings that the detritus once had.

The pieces are somewhat ephemeral and it is difficult to predict how long they will remain in one place. Documentation plays a huge role in my practice as it allows me to record the event and incorporate it into publications.

I bring only hints of the work back to the studio or gallery therefore in order to access the work you will need to download a free QR code scanner to your phone or device from the app store.

Naomi Proudlove

My work is a meditation on time as a way of making sense of what it means to be in the modern world and how we engage with the everyday existence. The repeated rhythm of daily life is observed through indexical action and object: traces of the body in motion recorded in an open-ended project of critical research. By engaging in the routine everyday, my work explores the dissolving distinction between art and environment, between process and finished work, and between inside and outside. The mundane repetitive activity of the domestic home, dominated by time-based routine, is juxtaposed with alternative temporal rhythms of the natural world in an attempt to engage with the world and question and consider what happens when nothing happens?

Lucy Ralph

I paint to tell stories, which I believe have the potential to reorganise the realm of visibility and bring awareness to the viewer what is beyond the limits of language. Whilst being inspired by great poets of the 20th Century, my paintings connect my own personal experiences, current world affairs and carefully selected objects to create fictional, timeless, placeless compositions that embody a sense of struggle and hope.
My fabricated, fragmented figures seem ghost-like to create an ironic distance with the viewer, depicting what we know about people whilst demonstrating the contemporary, chaotic and fragile state of humanity. By creating this distance, I aim to invite the viewer into a space to imagine and speculate, and this brings them closer to the sensitive realities that I am responding to in my paintings.
Often, I combine these reoccurring themes into installations of which I use a variety of materials, processes and objects, to work in a meaningful dialogue with particular paintings. Through the production of my practice, alongside continuous research and self reflection, I am constantly envisioning my next body of work.

Lucy Savage

1.'No. 1' Scrim, Latex, Steel Hook, Acrylic on Glass
2.'Panel' Acrylic on Glass
3.'No. 2' Human Hair, Scrim
4.'No. 3' Manx Sheep Wool, Scrim, Fishing Wire

My practice explores the internal machinery of the human body. Focusing on the intestine as an independent organism, I intend to express the instinctive and responsive material of the body.
Today we are rapidly immersing ourselves in the synthetic reality of the online world. As the virtual community expands, we are forgetting our physical presence and replacing the body with the device. Whilst this unfolds, our sensory experience of the world around us is deadened. I want to remind the viewer that they are a being of movement, sensation and instinct.
As a Manx artist I often employ materials from livestock specifically bred on the Isle of Man. Whilst growing up on the island I practiced drawing animals and humans alike, this encouraged me to regard the human as a primal and animalistic being.
My intestinal sculptures range specifically between 5 and 23ft, measuring the average difference between the small and large intestine. I often work in large-scale sculpture as a means of projecting myself as well as a method of exploring how size can effect the viewer’s experience of themselves in relation to the work.

With great thanks to the following businesses who have and continue to donate material to this practice…

Rush Hairdressing. Pentoville Rd, Kings Cross, London.
Pennybridge Stables. Kirk Michael. Isle of Man
Isle of Man Meats. Douglas, Isle of Man.

No animals have been harmed in the making of these works. It is important to me that all animal products are sourced from living animals on free-range farms. I am often involved in the extracting of hair material to ensure it is done safely and humanely.

Ellie Thurlwell

1.'HM 1905-06' Silkscreen on paper [image]

"We know that a picture is but a space in which a variety of images, none of them original, blend and clash. A picture is a tissue of quotations drawn from the innumerable centers of culture. Similar to those eternal copyists Bouvard and Pécuchet, we indicate the profound ridiculousness that is precisely the truth of painting. We can only imitate a gesture that is always anterior, never original. Succeeding the painter, the plagiarist no longer bears within them passions, humours, feelings, impressions, but rather this immense encyclopedia from which they draw. The viewer is the tablet on which all the quotations that make up a painting are inscribed without any of them being lost. A painting's meaning lies not in its origin, but in its destination. The birth of the viewer must be at the cost of the painter."
